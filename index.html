The Design and Development of Aira
- The Process

Initial Concept:

Commercial aerial autonomous vehicles as a means for multi-modal transportation.

Reducing noise and pollution, while increasing people's quality of life.

Existing Infrastructure

Public Transportation

Current city infrastructure has several issues, and part of the goal of my research was to gather data that tells us what people currently like and dislike about public transport in their city; and aim to provide all of the positives, and minimise as many negatives as we possibly can.

Uber

Uber's Elevate project aims to have test flights running within the next few years, meaning this form of autnomous air-taxi service is on it's way to major cities, and validate's a lot of our research and design.

Waymo + Tesla

Self driving technologies such as Google's Waymo and Tesla's line of electric cars are looking forward to what personal transport might be like in the future. 

Research

I interviewed 5 users of Sydney transport, including users of Uber. I chose to interview these people in order to guage public opinion on the various types of public transportation available in Sydney. This helped me gain deep insight into their experiences and develop empathy for the people I am designing a solution for.

In order to gather some initial quantitative public opinion data on the current state of Urban Transportation in Sydney, I ran a quick 1-minute questionnaire by people at wharfs, bus stops and train stations. 

Most people I interviewed referenced peak hour commutes, it was harder to find opinions about off-hour experiences, so I chose to do a contextual observation just before and after peak hour conditions in order to get accurate data from natural user behaviour for that time period.

Reflections on Research phase

I’ve collected and analysed a lot of data regarding the current percieved state of Sydney’s transport system. I have uncovered needs that are yet to be met by what both the public and private sectors are currently providing, and revealed key insights into what people like and dislike about their current commute. The interview stage was by far the most enlightening research and left me with a lot of qualitative data to work with. Using the affinity diagram I was able to synthesise it down into identifiable user needs. The other forms of research were less enlightening after conducting interviews as they seemed to mostly echo the data I had already acquired, or provide me with simply behavioural data that may come in use later on. In the future I would definately like to storyboard the experiences of other personas in order to create a more complete overview of the transport ecosystem. Finally, my research into Uber’s Elevate project has given me great anticipation and inspiration to get started on the next steps.

The other members of the team analysed:

What aspects of multi-modal transport to commuters prioritise?

Currently multi-modal transport does not provide enough choice, efficiency or wayfinding. How might we create the ideal door to door experience?

What is the relationship between lengthy commuting and quality of life?

Lengthy commuters suffer greatest from a constant lack of time that affects all aspects of their lives. How might we restore ownership of time back to commuters?

Narrowing our focus

After our initial brainstorm in which we branched out and ideated eight different concepts framed around our research on autonomous drones we decided to further explore AiraSolo, AiraShare, AiraScenic, AiraDrop and AiraHealth. We felt that these concepts had enough breadth for us to experiment with a variety of user interfaces and interactions to help later inform our design choices for our final chosen concept.

Reflection of Process

The methods we used in this section were successful in allowing us to quickly test the feasibility of our initial concepts. By exploring the user requirements through storyboarding, user flows and user stories we were able to gain a much clearer understanding of the functionality of each concept. Only once we understood this were we then able to consider which concepts we would actually be able to test with suitable participants. This was important as usability testing is only valuable if participants are representative of actual users. This process was highly beneficial to us, allowing us to test our concepts without spending too much time or resources. 

AiraHealth will not Progress

After further exploring these five concepts through storyboarding, user flows and user stories to further understand the user requirements and what functionality the user interface would have to support we decided not to progress AiraHealth into the next round of paper prototyping. We made this decision as our exploration of this concept made it clear to us that we lacked the background knowledge in this area and had limited access to appropriate participants which therefore would have hindered usability testing.

Low fidelity Paper Prototypes V1

Aim

Test a variety of interface styles using four different concepts to gain a high level understanding of what types of interfaces user’s find intuitive.

Method

Think Aloud Testing - Observe and listen to user’s interacting with an interface to understand what the pain points are and how to address them. Paper prototyping was used at this stage. We heavily asked for suggestions and had an open ended approach to further explore the opportunity space and identify further features for development in later iterations.

Evaluation Synthesis

Clearer iconography to identify AiraCraft’s on the map

Explain ‘Pick Up’ and ‘Drop Off’ better or change copy

Ability to toggle the visibility of AiraCraft’s on the map whilst flying

Reflection of the Process

This was our initial round of think aloud testing in which we tested four different concepts with four participants. They were relatively unstructured usability testing sessions with only a small number of set tasks, we placed the emphasis on trialling the concepts with participants to understand not just pain points. We filmed all tests and then reviewed the footage to take complete our observations and analysis. Whilst effective, this method was time consuming and we decided that in the next round we would also be taking notes throughout the testing to help speed up the process. Overall it we feel that it was a successful initial round of usability testing which we learnt a lot from.

Iteration Recommendations

Recommendation for the next round of AiraSolo iterations will be to change the placement of the passengers button so that users are able to select the number of passengers. Also to choose iconography that better depicts AiraCraft’s and illustrates clearly what ‘Pick Up’ and ‘Drop Off’ mean. The ability to toggle the visibility of AiraCrafts has also been noted as a recommendation with usability feedback mixed with some users enjoying it whilst others felt anxious about seeing this information.

Low fidelity Paper Prototypes V2

Aim

Test two prototypes with similar functionality but vastly different interface styles to learn how users interact with these different interaction models and how to apply them in the next round of iteration.

Method

Think Aloud Testing - Observe and listen to user’s interacting with an interface to understand what the pain points are and how to address them. Paper prototyping was used at this stage.

A/B Testing - Testing two different versions of the same product, to decide which one is more suitable for a specific user need. Shared screens between AiraSolo and AiraShare were strategically made with different interaction systems (card and circular) to test our assumptions and explore innovative UI.

Evaluation Synthesis

Need to provide duration for each leg of the journey on the route overview

Swiping through selection menus for each leg of the journey is tedious and cumbersome

Reflection of the Process

This round of usability testing was a lot more streamlined as we were only testing two concepts. As we had refined our paper prototypes with the intention of testing two very different UI layouts we created a set of concrete tasks and a series of think aloud tables to make it easier to analyse comparatively. We found this to more efficient than reviewing footage which we found to be too time consuming in the last round. By comparing the consistent screens across the two different interfaces we were able to understand user’s mental models and how we could explore bringing more intuitive card features into AiraSolo to potentially make the content more digestible.

Iteration Recommendations

The removal of the start screen was recommended as it simply added an extra button to click and instead could be implemented as a splash screen animation. Another recommendation was to consolidate the repetitive swipe selection of each leg of the journey into one screen as users found it frustrating. As the next iteration is digital it was also recommended to add a highlight colour to draw attention and clearly illustrate features such as the progress tracker bar which was not recognised well in testing. 

Low fidelity Prototypes V3

Aim

Test two versions of the same interface using a variety of qualitative and quantitative methods to learn which aspects were more successful, to iterate into our final interface. 

Method

Contextual Think Aloud Testing - Observe and listen to user’s interacting with an interface to understand what the issues are and how to address them. For this round, we placed all the prototypes on invision and used our phones for walkthroughs. To give further context, we gave users a background story & persona and brought them outside into the rain to test navigation and wayfinding in real environment scenarios.

A/B Testing - Testing two different versions of the same product, to decide which one is more suitable for a specific user need. We developed two solutions for AiraSolo with varying features to evaluate assumptions we made before creating the final low fidelity prototype.

Heuristic Evaluation - Identify usability problems in the UI using a set of design principles in order to find errors. We used Nielsen’s Heuristics as a framework.

System Usability Scale - An inexpensive, effective survey used for assessing the usability of a product or interface and identify issues. This provided a level of quantitative data for analysis of our A/B testing.

User Experience Questionnaire (Short) - Quickly assess the experience of the users of interactive products to facilitate iteration. This provided a level of quantitative data for analysis of our A/B testing.

Evaluation Synthesis

V3A had more complex and confusing navigation layout, it would be better to have the extra features hidden for more ‘expert’ users to customise

The linear progress tracker on V3B was easier for participants to understand

V3B needs to be imply swiping up more clearly to users

The location of the booking ticket in V3B was inconsistent and confusing whilst in V3A it was explicit

The progress bar times in V3A were labelled confusingly 

Reflection of the Process

In this stage we had two versions of one concept that we wanted to A/B test however as we did not have enough participants each tested both interfaces but we alternated the order in which they completed them to act as a control. In addition to the think aloud testing we also used the SUS and UEQ questionnaires to collect quantitative results to support our qualitative think alouds and aid our A/B analysis. Overall this round of testing was successful however after comparing the observations and questionnaire results it was apparent that the difficulty of the tasks were not evenly weighted between V3A and V3B and this may have caused some of the participants to view V3A more favourably in some aspects.

Iteration Recommendations

In our final iteration of AiraSolo we will move the boarding pass into the swipe up card so that it is consistently located with extra info rather than in the directions bar which was confusing. We also intend a cancel button so that users can exit from their trip any stage if needed and customise each set of cards specific to each leg. The rating screen also had recommendations to remove the progress line as it was redundant and to center and make larger the star rating system. We also will provide feedback that a rating has been submitted by incorporating the time saved this trip stats in a thank you message.

Reflection on V4

This iterative process was very intense and fast paced but very beneficial for all of us in honing our usability testing skills and how best to facilitate user testing in the future. Our user driven focus was pivotal to our rapid prototyping as we documented and synthesised all feedback given, iterating upon recommendations at every round of testing. We also learnt a lot about the balance between providing enough contextual information with our prototypes in order to convey our ideas at such a low fidelity. Overall this process has been highly rewarding and we look forward to conducting more usability testing in the future as insights gained from them are extremely helpful.

Next Steps

Our next steps will be to increase the fidelity of our prototype and then conduct another round of usability testing using heuristic evaluations and the think aloud method and potentially set up remote testing so that more quantitative data can be collected. From synthesising the results of our testing we then intend to iterate again and move into the interactive prototyping phase. If time allows it would be greatly beneficial for us to conduct more think aloud testing but if not we will utilize heuristic evaluations to iterate our design until we feel it offers to most complete user experience.

High Fidelity Redesign Reflection

This iteration of our design was the result of our bold decision to overhaul the circular design of our previous iterations, and replace it with the card-based UI we experimented with in our testing of the AiraShare concept. It retains all the functionality of our previous AiraSolo design, including compass navigation, but removes a lot of unused white space from the screen in order to provide the user with a cleaner and more complete experience.

In our metamorphosis into this design we experimented with several UI designs, for settings menus, trip selection and even search suggestions; everything was overhauled and revised for peak usability according to the data we had gathered during our user testing and evaluations.
